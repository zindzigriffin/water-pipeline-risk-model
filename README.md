Water Distribution Pipeline Condition Assessment & Failure Prediction
A machine learning framework for predicting pipe failure likelihood and prioritizing infrastructure inspection across municipal water distribution systems
Can a machine learning model identify which pipes are most likely to fail before they do  and tell you exactly why?

What This Is
At its core this is a supervised classification and risk scoring study not of water quality outputs but of infrastructure condition signals. The question is not whether a pipe is currently leaking. It is whether the operational and physical attributes of a pipe segment encode its failure risk at all and if so which specific factors are driving that prediction.
This repository contains:

A self-contained analysis script water_pipeline_model.py walking through the full data pipeline, model training, evaluation, and risk scoring.
A 9-panel visualization pipeline_analysis.png generated directly from the model outputs showing ROC curves, feature importance, failure rates by material, district-level risk maps, and model comparison.
A synthetic dataset modeled on EPA ECHO attribute distributions and ASCE infrastructure failure studies, fully swappable for real utility data.

Why This Research Exists
Every year water utilities across the United States experience thousands of main breaks. Cast iron pipes installed before 1950 corrode silently. Asbestos cement mains crack under pressure fluctuations. Aging infrastructure fails not all at once but through a slow accumulation of stress: each prior break, each spike in operating pressure, each year without inspection nudging a segment closer to failure.
Water utility engineers can visually inspect some pipes. But they cannot inspect all of them. And when they must decide which segments to prioritize for replacement or assessment, they are often working from spreadsheets, institutional memory, and reactive data — responding to breaks that have already happened rather than anticipating ones that have not.
Machine learning changes that calculus. The question this project asks is whether the physical and operational attributes of a pipe — its age, its material, its break history, the corrosivity of the soil around it — contain enough signal to predict failure risk before it manifests. And if they do, which attributes matter most?

The Core Problem with Infrastructure Risk Assessment
When utilities manage aging pipe networks they face a hidden prioritization problem: every segment looks low-risk until it fails. Inspection budgets are finite. Replacement cycles span decades. Without a systematic scoring framework, capital decisions default to recency bias — fixing what just broke rather than preventing what is about to.
Two pipe segments can have identical diameters and the same installation decade. One runs through highly corrosive soil with two prior breaks and no recent inspection. The other runs through low-corrosivity ground with a clean service record. Do standard asset management tools see a difference? And if so which attributes are actually driving that risk score?
Without answering this, utilities are making million-dollar capital decisions on an unexamined foundation.

What This Project Does
This project applies supervised machine learning to a domain where interpretability is as important as accuracy: municipal water infrastructure condition assessment.
Rather than treating the risk model as a black box that outputs a score, this project opens it up and asks: which specific pipe attributes drive the classification boundary between low-risk and high-risk segments? Is that signal consistent across model architectures? Can it be measured, ranked, and acted on by a field engineer?
This is feature-engineered binary classification with full model comparison and district-level risk aggregation. Pipe segments are characterized by 12 engineered attributes and the model measures whether high-risk and low-risk segments are linearly separable in that feature space. The methodology then identifies exactly which dimensions are driving the decision boundary.

The Methodology
The dataset contains 2,000 pipe segments modeled on real-world municipal water infrastructure attributes drawn from EPA ECHO database distributions and ASCE Infrastructure Report Card failure studies. Each segment is characterized by:

Physical attributes: pipe material, diameter, installation year, pipe age
Operational attributes: operating pressure (psi), prior break count, leak complaints
Condition signals: soil corrosivity rating, tuberculation index, years since last inspection
Engineered features: age–pressure interaction term, break rate per decade, inspection lag risk score

Failure labels were derived from a probabilistic ground truth function grounded in field-validated risk weights for each material class and soil corrosivity level — the same risk factors cited in ASCE and EPA infrastructure condition literature.

AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve) measures how well a model separates high-risk from low-risk segments across all possible classification thresholds. A score of 1.0 means perfect separation. A score of 0.5 means the model performs no better than a coin flip.
The finding: all three architectures achieve strong separation, confirming that pipe failure risk is genuinely encoded in the physical and operational attributes — this is not noise. Logistic Regression edges out the ensemble methods on test AUC while providing the most transparent coefficient-level interpretability, making it the preferred choice for a utility context where engineers need to explain and defend prioritization decisions.

Isolating the Risk Drivers
By ranking which attributes shift most dramatically between low-risk and high-risk classifications, the feature importance analysis surfaces what functions as a dedicated failure signal in this dataset.
Pipe age and the age–pressure interaction term emerge as the primary drivers — older pipes under higher operating pressure accumulate stress in a way that neither variable captures alone. Prior break count and break rate per decade form the second tier, confirming that break history is not just a lagging indicator but a predictive one. Tuberculation index and soil corrosivity round out the circuit, consistent with field literature on corrosion-driven main failures.
Material class produces the starkest failure rate differential: Cast Iron and Asbestos Cement segments fail at rates more than 2× higher than PVC and Ductile Iron, directly matching the replacement priority guidance in ASCE's Infrastructure Report Card.

Why Interpretable Models Matter for Infrastructure Decisions
Most ML deployments optimize for accuracy. Infrastructure risk assessment also requires justifiability.

Capital decisions require audit trails. If a model recommends replacing a pipeline segment, the utility engineer presenting that recommendation to a board needs to explain which attributes drove it — not cite a black-box score.
Failure modes must be traceable. When a segment flagged as low-risk later fails, the post-mortem requires understanding what the model missed and why.
Equity considerations are real. Pipe age and material class correlate with neighborhood infrastructure investment history. A model that cannot explain its recommendations cannot be audited for disparate service impacts.


The Practical Application: Inspection Prioritization
The immediate output of this framework is a district-level risk dashboard aggregating predicted failure scores across service areas. The top 10 highest-risk segments are surfaced directly, along with the specific attribute profile driving each score.
The practical workflow: a pipeline infrastructure engineer ingests current asset management data, runs the scoring model, and receives a ranked list of segments exceeding a configurable risk threshold — enabling proactive inspection scheduling and capital planning rather than reactive break response.
A risk threshold calibrated at a predicted failure probability of 0.55 using this framework's ground truth function surfaces the segments where multiple high-weight risk factors converge: old Cast Iron or Asbestos Cement mains, elevated operating pressure, prior break history, and extended inspection gaps.

What Is Next
The current phase establishes the feature architecture, model benchmarks, and risk scoring framework using synthetic data modeled on real infrastructure attributes. The next phase is integration with a real utility dataset — either EBMUD's publicly available water quality and infrastructure reports or EPA ECHO's SDWIS data — to validate the feature importance rankings against observed break records and calibrate the risk threshold against actual failure outcomes.
